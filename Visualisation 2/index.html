What is Australia’s Effect on Global CO₂ Emissions?

The CO₂ Challenge: Why it Matters to you

Understanding CO₂ at a glance

Carbon dioxide (CO₂) is the main greenhouse gas driving climate change, which affects everything from bushfire intensity and droughts to extreme heat and rising sea levels. In Australia, these impacts are felt directly in our daily lives - from hotter summers and more dangerous fire seasons to threats to agriculture, water supply, and iconic ecosystems like the Great Barrier Reef.

CO₂ primarily comes from burning fossil fuels - coal, oil and gas - as well as industrial activities such as cement production and land-use change. Australia’s economy, with its mining, energy, and transport sectors, contributes to emissions that are high on a per-person basis, even if the country’s total share of global emissions is relatively modest.

Globally, emissions rose from about 24 billion tonnes in 2000 to roughly 37 billion tonnes by 2023. This dashboard uses a dataset covering the years 2000 to 2023 to give a clear picture of how emissions have changed over time, both globally and for Australia

The visualisations include a global perspective - showing how countries like China, the US, India, and European nations compare - alongside a detailed view of Australia’s emissions. Australians can explore annual trends, understand the breakdown of emissions by source, and see how our per-person footprint compares to other nations. The dashboard brings these numbers to life through interactive charts and maps, making it easier to grasp complex information at a glance.

By exploring this dashboard, everyday Australians can better understand the scale of the climate challenge, see where progress is being made, and identify areas where change is needed - whether at the national level, in local communities, or in our own choices at home and on the road.

Global Hotspots: Who’s Emitting What

Visualising global emissions

The choropleth map shows CO₂ emissions by country from 2000 to 2023. Dark red areas indicate higher emissions, while lighter shades show lower levels.

China’s emissions surged from ~3,200 Mt in 2000 to > 12,000 Mt in 2023, making it the largest global emitter.

The United States shows a decline from ~6,000 Mt to ~5,000 Mt, reflecting energy efficiency improvements and reduced coal use. India and Indonesia show rapid growth, while Europe (e.g., Germany, UK) steadily declines due to policy and renewable adoption.

The Race for Emissions: Top Countries in Motion

Top emitters over time

This animated bar chart shows the top 10 CO₂-emitting countries from 2000 to 2023. Each bar represents annual emissions in million tonnes (Mt).

China dominates with rapid industrial expansion, rising from ~3,200 Mt to >12,000 Mt, overtaking the US in the mid-2000s. The United States gradually declines from ~6,000 Mt to just over 5,000 Mt, reflecting cleaner energy adoption and reduced coal use.

India more than doubles emissions (1,000 → 3,500 Mt), while emerging economies like Indonesia, Iran, and Saudi Arabia rise into the top ranks. Europe (Germany, UK) declines steadily, showing effects of efficiency and renewables.

Australia consistently appears in the top 15, demonstrating its carbon-intensive economy despite being mid-range in absolute terms.

Tracking Trends: How the World and Australia Stack Up

Global Trends and Australia’s Context

These two line charts illustrate how global CO₂ emissions have evolved since 2000. The first chart shows total national emissions, highlighting the rapid industrial expansion of China — increasing from ~3,200 Mt in 2000 to > 12,000 Mt in 2023, overtaking the United States, which slightly declined from ~6,000 Mt to just above 5,000 Mt.

India’s emissions more than doubled (1,000 → 3,500 Mt), while Russia and Japan remained relatively stable over the period.

Australia’s total emissions stayed between ~370–420 Mt, accounting for ~1% of global emissions. This places Australia far below major economies in absolute terms, yet its emissions have not fallen significantly despite global decarbonisation efforts.

The second chart focuses on CO₂ per capita. While the global average in 2023 is ~4.6 t/person, Australia exceeds 15 t/person, more than three times the global mean and higher than most developed nations.

Even as countries like the US and Japan reduced per-capita emissions, Australia’s values remain persistently high. This divergence emphasizes structural challenges from its coal-heavy energy mix and fossil fuel exports, highlighting the importance of renewable adoption to align with global decarbonisation trends.

Australia Under the Microscope: Where Our CO₂ Comes From

Detailed Source Breakdown

This pie chart illustrates the composition of Australia’s total CO₂ emissions in 2023, showing its continued reliance on fossil fuels.

Coal remains the dominant contributor, largely due to electricity generation and export-oriented mining. Oil and gas follow as major sources, driven by transportation, industrial use, and energy production.

Smaller segments include cement production, flaring, and land-use change, highlighting industrial and agricultural contributions to the carbon footprint.

While renewable energy capacity is expanding, fossil fuels still underpin most of Australia’s energy system.

Total emissions: ~400 Mt CO₂, roughly 1% of global emissions. Despite the modest absolute share, Australia ranks among the highest per capita worldwide. The breakdown underscores structural challenges for decarbonising a resource-intensive economy and the urgent need for cleaner energy transitions, especially in power and transport.

Key Insights: What This Means for Australia

Australia’s CO₂ story shows a country with modest absolute emissions -1% of global emissions, but a persistently high per-capita footprint, reflecting its coal-heavy energy system, fossil fuel exports, and transport and industrial reliance on carbon-intensive fuels.

From 2000 to 2023, global emissions grew sharply, driven by industrial expansion in countries like China and India, while some nations such as the US and parts of Europe have reduced their totals and per-capita emissions through efficiency and renewable adoption. Australia’s total emissions remained relatively flat during this period, but per-person emissions remain among the highest in the world, highlighting the country’s structural challenges.

The visualisations in this dashboard make these patterns clear — global trends, national totals, per-capita comparisons, and Australia’s detailed emission breakdown by source — helping everyday Australians see where progress is being made and where action is still urgently needed.

Effective decarbonisation in Australia will require: a continued shift to renewable energy, improvements in energy efficiency, modernization of industry, and targeted policy interventions in power generation and transport. Understanding both absolute and per-capita perspectives is key to making informed choices at the national, community, and personal level.
